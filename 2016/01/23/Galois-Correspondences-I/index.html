Galois Correspondences I

Posted on 2016-01-23 Edited on 2022-10-01 In Math

In this post, we discuss the fundamental theorem of Galois theory along the consideration of Galois connections.

Galois Connections

Definition: An (antitone) Galois connection is a dual adjunction between posets. In other words, a Galois connection consists of the following data:

  • Two posets A,BA, BA,B,
  • Two order-reversing maps f:A→Bf:A\to Bf:A→B and g:B→Ag:B\to Ag:B→A ,
    such that
  • a≤g(f(a))a\le g(f(a))a≤g(f(a)) and b≤f(g(b))b\le f(g(b))b≤f(g(b)) for all a∈Aa\in Aa∈A, b∈Bb\in Bb∈B.

One can see that fff and ggg automatically satisfy the triangle identities: $$f\circ g\circ f = f, \qquad g\circ f\circ g = g,$$ and thus form a dual adjunction between posets.

Definition: An (antitone) Galois correspondence is a dual adjoint equivalence between posets, that is a Galois connection such that f∘g=1f\circ g = 1f∘g=1 and g∘f=1g\circ f = 1g∘f=1.

One can verify the following basic fact.
Proposision: Any Galois connection (A,B,f,g)(A,B,f,g)(A,B,f,g) induces a Galois correspondence (g(B),f(A),f,g)(g(B),f(A),f,g)(g(B),f(A),f,g).

Recall that a dual adjoint functor maps colimits to limits and gives canonical morphisms from colimits to the image of limits. Thus we have
Proposision: Let (A,B,f,g)(A,B,f,g)(A,B,f,g) be a Galois connection. Then for any ai∈A,bj∈Ba_i\in A, b_j\in Bai​∈A,bj​∈B, we have

\begin{align*} f(\sup \{a_i\}) = \inf\{f(a_i)\}, &\quad& f(\inf\{a_i\}) \ge \sup\{f(a_i)\},\\ g(\sup\{b_j\}) = \inf\{g(b_j)\}, &\quad& g(\inf\{b_j\}) \ge \sup\{g(b_j)\}. \end{align*}

Remark: One can also consider the notions of monotone Galois connections and monotone Galois correspondences.

Galois Groups and Galois Extensions

Now we apply the above to algebraic field extensions. So throughout this section, every field extension is assumed to be algebraic.

First Galois Correspondence

First, we have the folloing Galois connection:

\begin{align*} \{\text{subgroups of }\mathrm{Aut}(K)\} & \longleftrightarrow \{\text{subfields of }K\}.\\ G & \longrightarrow K^G \\ \mathrm{Gal}(K/F) & \longleftarrow F. \end{align*}

Here we use Gal(K/F)\mathrm{Gal}(K/F)Gal(K/F) to denote the group of automorphisms of KKK fixing FFF.

We define
Definition: A subgroup GGG of Aut(K)\mathrm{Aut}(K)Aut(K) is called a Galois group if G=Gal(K/F)G=\mathrm{Gal}(K/F)G=Gal(K/F), where FFF is a subfield of KKK. A field extension K/FK/FK/F is called a Galois extension if F=KGF=K^GF=KG, where GGG is a subgroup of Aut(K)\mathrm{Aut}(K)Aut(K).

Then we obtain the Galois corresponding between Galois groups and Galois extensions. However, this correpondence is not the one the fundamental theorem of Galois theory states. To get it, we need to look closer. For the field extension side, we need to show that if K/FK/FK/F is a Galois extension, then so are all K/EK/EK/E where EEE is an intermediate field of K/FK/FK/F. This follows immediately once one proves the following lemma.

Lemma: A field extension is a Galois extension if and only if it is the splitting field of a family of separable polynomials.
Note that, from this, one can see any Galois extension must be a separable extension.

For the subgroup side, one only need to notice that the Galois subgroups must be closed. Then, we get the Galois correspondence for a Galois extension K/FK/FK/F:

\begin{align*} \{\text{closed subgroups of }\mathrm{Gal}(K/F)\} & \longleftrightarrow \{\text{intermediate fields of }K/F\}.\\ H & \longrightarrow K^H\\ \mathrm{Gal}(K/E) & \longleftarrow E. \end{align*}

Note that under this corresponding, open subgroups of Gal(K/F)\mathrm{Gal}(K/F)Gal(K/F) correspond finite subextensions of K/FK/FK/F.

Second Galois Correspondence

Now, we restrict the map H↦KHH\mapsto K^HH↦KH to the subposet of normal subgroups of Gal(K/F)\mathrm{Gal}(K/F)Gal(K/F) and identify it with the opposite of the poset of quotient of Gal(K/F)\mathrm{Gal}(K/F)Gal(K/F). Then, we have the following order-reversing map

\begin{align*} \{\text{quotients of }\mathrm{Gal}(K/F)\} & \longleftrightarrow \{\text{intermediate fields of }K/F\}.\\ \mathrm{Gal}(K/F)/H & \longmapsto K^H. \end{align*}

One can see

Gal(KH/F)=Gal(K/F)/H.\mathrm{Gal}(K^H/F) = \mathrm{Gal}(K/F)/H. Gal(KH/F)=Gal(K/F)/H.

In other word, Gal(KH/F)\mathrm{Gal}(K^H/F)Gal(KH/F) is a quotient of Gal(K/F)\mathrm{Gal}(K/F)Gal(K/F) with kernel HHH.

For an arbitrary field extension E/FE/FE/F, there is no need to be an epimorphism

Gal(F)⟶Gal(E/F).\mathrm{Gal}(F)\longrightarrow\mathrm{Gal}(E/F). Gal(F)⟶Gal(E/F).

Here Gal(F)\mathrm{Gal}(F)Gal(F) denotes the absolutely Galois group Gal(Falg/F)\mathrm{Gal}(F_{\mathrm{alg}}/F)Gal(Falg​/F).

Note that, Gal(Falg/F)=Gal(Fsep/F)\mathrm{Gal}(F_{\mathrm{alg}}/F) = \mathrm{Gal}(F_{\mathrm{sep}}/F)Gal(Falg​/F)=Gal(Fsep​/F), where FsepF_{\mathrm{sep}}Fsep​ is the separable closure of FFF and Fsep/FF_{\mathrm{sep}}/FFsep​/F is always Galois.

We define
Definition: A field extension E/FE/FE/F is call a normal extension if any embedding E→FalgE\to F_{\mathrm{alg}}E→Falg​ induces an automorphism of EEE over FFF.
One can see that Galois = normal + separable if one has known the following lemma.
Lemma: A field extension is a normal extension if and only if it is the splitting field of a family of polynomials.

Now, one can now see that if E/FE/FE/F is a normal extension, then there is a group homomorphism

\begin{align*} \mathrm{Gal}(F) & \longrightarrow \mathrm{Gal}(E/F). \\\ \sigma & \longmapsto \sigma|_E, \end{align*}

As any automorphism of a normal extension E/FE/FE/F can be extended into an automorphism of Falg/FF_{\mathrm{alg}}/FFalg​/F, the above homomorphism is surjective. The kernel of it is obviously the absolutely Galois group Gal(E)\mathrm{Gal}(E)Gal(E).

In this way, we get a Galois connection:

\begin{align*} \{\text{quotients of }\mathrm{Gal}(F)\} & \longleftrightarrow \{\text{normal extensions of }F\}.\\ \mathrm{Gal}(F)/H & \longrightarrow F_{\mathrm{alg}}^H\\ \mathrm{Gal}(E) & \longleftarrow E. \end{align*}

Let K/FK/FK/F be another normal extension such that E⊂KE\subset KE⊂K. Then, by the Galois connection, we have

Gal(Falg/K)<Gal(Falg/E).\mathrm{Gal}(F_{\mathrm{alg}}/K)< \mathrm{Gal}(F_{\mathrm{alg}}/E). Gal(Falg​/K)<Gal(Falg​/E).

Therefore there is a homomorphism

\mathrm{Gal}(K/F)\longrightarrow\mathrm{Gal}(E/F). $$ fitting into the following commutative diagram

\begin{gather*}
\mathrm{Gal}(F_{\mathrm{alg}}/F) \
\swarrow\qquad\qquad\searrow \
\mathrm{Gal}(K/F)\longrightarrow\mathrm{Gal}(E/F)
\end{gather*}

and hence an epimorphism. In particular this holds for Galois extensions $K/F$. In this way, we get a Galois correspondence:

\begin{align*}
{\text{quotients of }\mathrm{Gal}(K/F)} & \longleftrightarrow
{\text{normal subextensions of }K/F}.\
\mathrm{Gal}(K/F)/H &
\longrightarrow K^H \
\mathrm{Gal}(K/E) &
\longleftarrow E.
\end{align*}

### The Degree Encoding Let $K/F$ be a field extension. The poset of closed subgroups of $G=\mathrm{Gal}(K/F)$ admits an order-preserving map $H\mapsto|H|$ and an order-reversing map $H\mapsto[G:H]$ to the poset of *supernatural numbers*. Moreover, we have

|G| = |H|\cdot[G:H].

Likewise, the poset of intermediate fields $E$ of $K/F$ admits an order-reversing map $E\mapsto[K:E]$ and an order-preserving map $E\mapsto[E:F]$ to the poset of supernatural numbers and we have

[K:F] = [K:E]\cdot[E:F].

The Galois correspondences suggest us to compare them. This is what Artin's theorem says. **Artin's Theorem:** Let $K$ be a field and $G$ a closed subgroup of $\mathrm{Aut}(K)$. Then

|G| = [K:K^G].

Note that when apply to Galois extensions $K/F$, the theorem implies

|H| = [K:K^H],
\quad\text{and}\quad
[G:H] = [K^H:F].
